## 1a. Official Problem-Statement Deck Review (2026-07-08)
After v1.3 scored only 0.289 (a weak +0.014 gain over v1.2, far below the ~0.94 competitor cluster), the official Amex Campus Challenge Round 1 problem-statement deck (`campus_challenge26_r1.pdf`, provided at competition kickoff) was reviewed directly rather than continuing to guess variable signs blind. Key findings:

- **`feature_desc.csv` (the f1-f23 name mapping used throughout this entire project) is confirmed to be an official file provided by Amex/Unstop**, not a guessed/reverse-engineered mapping. All prior variable-meaning work in this log stands.
- **Official evaluation confirmed**: accuracy = % overlap between predicted top-20% and actual top-20% profitable cardmembers; 70% public / 30% private hidden split; max 10 submissions per team; public leaderboard is rank-ordered by best score attained.
- **Official objective, verbatim**: "design a framework or equation to quantify cardmember profitability to issuer by incorporating revenues and costs, in order to identify and prioritize the most profitable customers" — confirms the Revenue−Cost framing used since Section 1 of this project.
- **Official Card Member Attribute Categories** (the deck's own grouping of the ~23 masked attributes, given as illustrative examples per category, not an exhaustive list):
  - **CM Spend & Balance**: industry level spends, revolve behavior
  - **Benefit Usage**: no. of lounge visits, no. of months cab credits are utilized
  - **Engagement**: no. of calls to cancel premier card, website logins, # of cards held
  - **Profile**: tenure, riskiness score
- **Actionable finding #1 — f6-f10 miscategorized in our own formula work.** "Industry level spends" is officially part of **CM Spend & Balance** (the same revenue category as Total Spend and Revolve Balance), not the "rewards-ecosystem-linked auxiliary signal, exclude it" story built during the Section K deep dive. That deep dive was a reasonable inference from correlation patterns alone (f6-f10 don't sum to f5, correlate with f4/f21 rewards variables) but is superseded by the official categorization. f6-f10 had been excluded from every formula version (v1 through v1.3) on this now-outdated reasoning — a potentially significant miss given their large raw magnitude (f7 alone averages ~₹30,822, vs. f5's ~₹3,465).
- **Actionable finding #2 — f19 categorized under Engagement, in tension with our risk-amplifier finding.** "# of cards held" (f19/f20) sits under **Engagement**, which typically implies a positive profitability relationship (deeper relationship = more valuable customer) — contradicting the risk-amplifier (negative) reframing this project derived empirically (Sections 8/9, B3, Row-Level Validation). Our empirical evidence was fairly robust (row-level lift, confirmed after controlling for has_lend_line), so this isn't being reversed outright, but it's flagged as unresolved tension worth testing directly.
- **"Tenure" (Profile category) — not a contradiction of the earlier "no tenure proxy exists" finding.** The category slide explicitly says "~23 attributes spread across different categories" and lists only 9 example items total across all 4 categories — clearly illustrative, not exhaustive. "Riskiness score" maps cleanly to f11; "Tenure" most likely either didn't make it into this specific masked dataset as a standalone column, or is implicitly captured by attributes already in use (e.g. f17/f18 lend line size, which is exactly why it was tested as an imperfect tenure substitute in Section N/A2). Not treated as evidence that `feature_desc.csv` is incomplete or wrong.
- **Formula v1.4 acts on both actionable findings** — see Section 6.

## 13. v1 Formula Calibration Evidence — 2026-07-08
Quantitative validation of the 6 open decisions in the exact v1 spec (Section 6), computed on the real 500K-row dataset (`df_clean`, imputed). All transforms computed once as spec'd: `pctrank(f5)`, `pctrank(f1)`, `pctrank(f11)`, `pctrank(lend_exposure)` where `lend_exposure = mean(f17,f18)`.

### 13a. Percentile rank vs z-score
Both transforms collapse identically at each column's cap (all capped rows tie — 1 distinct value either way; e.g. f11's 12,837 capped rows give exactly 1 z-score and 1 pctrank). The real difference is in the top 10%: pctrank's span is fixed at ~0.087-0.098 for every variable (f5, f1, f11, lend_exposure alike), while z-score's span varies 1.17-2.57 depending on each variable's skew. **Verdict: pctrank is the right choice for v1** — a fixed weight (e.g. 0.35) means the same thing across all four terms under pctrank, whereas under z-score the same weight would swing the score by more than 2x depending on which variable it's multiplying (0.35×1.17≈0.41 for f5's top decile vs 0.35×2.57≈0.90 for f11's). Switching to z-score would silently distort the intended weight ratios.

### 13b. Risk penalty exponent — decile-level fit
Clean equal-sized deciles (tie-broken via `rank(method='first')`) show the pullback in mean `lend_exposure` is flat/noisy through decile 8 (most of that range is tied at f11=0, so ordering is arbitrary), then drops sharply: 55.0% of the total D1→D10 pullback happens between decile 8 and 9 alone, reaching 100% by decile 10. Fitting linear/squared/cubic candidates against the actual per-decile pullback shape (SSE): linear=25,791, squared=12,764, **cubic=7,607 (best fit)**. Even cubic undershoots the real sharpness (predicts 71.6% at decile 9 vs actual 55%, meaning the true curve is closer to a step/threshold function than any smooth power curve tested). **Recommendation: change the risk-penalty exponent from ^2 to ^3** — evidence-based improvement; a threshold/step function is a candidate for v2 if tighter fidelity is needed.

### 13c. M1 (lend-exposure override) — real effect on top-20% selection
Danger zone recomputed exactly per spec (pctrank(lend_exposure)≥0.75 AND pctrank(f11)≥0.75) on the full 500K population: **21,505 rows** — larger than Section 12's 4,448 because that figure was computed only among has_lend_line=1 customers (~190K), while the v1 spec computes pctrank "once, on the full 500K rows," which shifts the 75th-percentile threshold down. Restricting to the has_lend_line=1 subgroup for a like-for-like comparison gives 4,805 rows, consistent with Section 12's scale. **Under both population definitions, 0 danger-zone customers cross into the top 20% with or without M1 applied** — the squared (or, per 13b, cubic) risk penalty in Base already excludes this entire group before M1 fires. Closest miss: 0.003 below the top-20% threshold (full-population version), 0.03 below (restricted version). **M1 does zero marginal work for top-20% selection as currently used** — it may still matter for ordering within the bottom 80% (e.g. a future "worst-tier" cutoff), but isn't validated as pulling anyone out of the top 20% who wouldn't already be excluded.

### 13d. M2/M3 weight derivation from row-level lift
